What Happened Today in AI

Big tech is slashing jobs as it pivots to AI — and ABC News is asking directly whether a wipeout is coming. An AI company struck a power deal with a Tasmanian state generator, raising questions about who gets energy priority in Australia. Mantis Biotech is building digital twins of humans for drug development. Mistral raised $830M for a Paris data centre. A new poll found only 15% of Americans would willingly work for an AI boss. And trust in AI results is falling even as usage climbs. A day of reckoning across workforce, infrastructure, and safety.

Is AI Going to Cause a Job Wipeout?

ABC News ran a major investigation into whether AI-driven automation is accelerating job losses beyond what is being officially acknowledged. Big tech companies — Microsoft, Google, Meta — are cutting thousands of roles while simultaneously reporting record profits. The implicit argument is that AI is doing the work of workers who have been removed. The Australian angle is acute: white-collar roles in financial services, legal, and professional services are already being restructured, and resources and manufacturing are watching closely.

  • Source: ABC News Australia — Is AI going to cause a job wipeout?

AI Deal with Tasmania Power Generator Questioned

An AI company has struck a long-term power purchase agreement with a Tasmanian state electricity generator. Critics are questioning whether dedicated power supply for AI data centres is appropriate when industrial users and households are competing for grid capacity. Tasmania's hydro-heavy grid makes it attractive for AI infrastructure seeking low-carbon energy claims, but the deal raises sovereignty and priority questions about who controls critical energy resources.

  • Source: ABC News Australia — AI deal with Tasmania power generator questioned

Mantis Biotech Building "Digital Twins" of Humans

Mantis Biotech has announced a platform for creating computational digital twins of individual patients, primarily for pharmaceutical drug development and personalised medicine. The platform models how a specific patient's biology will respond to candidate compounds, reducing the need for certain trial phases. This directly intersects with bioprocess engineering, formulation science, and manufacturing process design in pharma.

  • Source: TechCrunch — Mantis Biotech digital twins

Mistral AI Raises $830M in Debt for Paris Data Centre

Mistral AI, Europe's leading open-weight model company, raised $830M in debt financing to fund a major Paris data centre. This signals that the open-source AI model ecosystem is now competing at infrastructure scale with US hyperscalers. For engineers using open-weight models in industrial settings, this investment suggests continued long-term support for Mistral's model family.

  • Source: TechCrunch — Mistral AI $830M

Only 15% of Americans Willing to Work for an AI Boss

A new Pew Research poll found that just 15% of Americans would be comfortable reporting to an AI system rather than a human manager. The number rises to 28% for younger workers aged 18–29, but remains strikingly low given how rapidly AI is being integrated into workplace decision-making. Resistance to AI authority is high — which matters for how AI tools are deployed in process control rooms and plant operations.

  • Source: TechCrunch — AI boss poll

Qodo Raises $70M for AI Code Verification

Qodo, an AI startup focused on verifying the correctness of AI-generated code, raised $70M. The core product checks whether AI-written code actually does what it claims to do — a crucial capability as AI-generated code enters safety-critical domains including industrial control systems, SCADA platforms, and process automation. The funding reflects a growing market recognition that AI code generation without verification is a liability.

  • Source: TechCrunch — Qodo $70M

Fewer Americans Trust AI Results Even as Adoption Grows

A new survey from the Reuters Institute found that trust in AI-generated information is declining in the US even as overall usage of AI tools increases. The pattern — use more, trust less — reflects a maturing awareness of AI limitations, hallucinations, and inconsistency. For safety engineers, this "trust gap" is not irrational; it reflects exactly the kind of critical thinking required when deploying AI recommendations in high-stakes environments.

  • Source: TechCrunch — AI trust gap report

Who's Impacted Today

Process Engineers & Plant Operators — The Job Wipeout Question

The ABC News job wipeout investigation is the most directly relevant story for anyone studying or working in Australian chemical engineering. Here is what the evidence actually shows for process industries:

  • Routine monitoring, report generation, shift handover documentation, and data entry in process plants are already being compressed by AI — these are the tasks most likely to see headcount reduction first
  • Process engineering roles tied to optimisation, troubleshooting, and HAZOP facilitation are more defensible. AI can assist, but the liability and judgement burden stays with licensed engineers
  • In Australian mining (BHP, Fortescue, Rio Tinto) and LNG (Woodside, Santos), autonomous operations are eliminating some operator roles while creating new "AI systems operations" roles. The net picture is not straightforwardly negative — but the transition is real and fast
  • For ChemE students at Melbourne, Monash, UQ, or UNSW: the engineers most at risk are those doing only the tasks that AI does well. The engineers who will thrive are those who can validate AI recommendations, design the systems AI operates within, and take responsibility for outcomes
  • Try this: Look at three recent job postings for process engineers in Australia on LinkedIn. Count how many mention AI, digital tools, or data skills. Compare with postings from two years ago.

Pharmaceutical Manufacturing Engineers — Mantis Biotech Digital Twins

The Mantis Biotech announcement is significant for anyone in bioprocess or pharmaceutical manufacturing engineering. Digital twins of biological systems are already used in fermentation process design — Mantis is extending this concept to the patient level.

  • For Australian pharma manufacturers like CSL Behring (Broadmeadows), Pfizer (Melbourne), and Seqirus, digital twin technology is becoming a competitive capability in process development. The ability to simulate how upstream bioreactor conditions affect downstream product quality — and ultimately patient outcomes — is a direct extension of what process engineers do
  • The FDA and TGA are increasingly receptive to digital twin evidence in regulatory submissions. Chemical engineers who understand both the biology and the computational modelling will be valuable in filing manufacturing process validation dossiers
  • Bioprocess optimisation using digital twins reduces the number of costly physical experiments needed during process development. This compresses development timelines and reduces batch failure risk
  • Try this: Search for "digital twin bioreactor" on Google Scholar. Look at how validated computational fluid dynamics (CFD) models are being used to replace some physical scale-up experiments.

Energy Engineers & Industrial Users — Tasmania AI Power Deal

The Tasmania power deal story matters well beyond one state's electricity market. It represents a structural tension that will define Australian industrial energy policy for the next decade.

  • AI data centres consume enormous quantities of electricity — a large hyperscale facility can use as much power as a small city. When an AI company secures priority access to a state generator's capacity, it competes directly with industrial users including chemical plants, aluminium smelters, and water treatment facilities
  • Tasmania's hydro-heavy grid is low-carbon and highly desirable for AI companies making net-zero commitments. But Hydro Tasmania also serves Bell Bay industrial users including Nyrstar zinc smelting and Norske Skog paper operations, which have historically depended on competitive electricity pricing
  • For energy engineers at Australian chemical plants, AI infrastructure deals could tighten grid capacity and push industrial electricity prices up — a direct operating cost impact
  • On the other side, AI-driven grid management and demand response systems are being piloted in South Australia and Victoria to improve renewable integration. The same AI expansion that pressures industrial energy users also enables smarter grid operation
  • Try this: Look up AEMO's (Australian Energy Market Operator) 2025–2026 Integrated System Plan. Find where data centres and large new loads appear in their demand forecasts.

Safety & Process Control Engineers — Code Verification and the Trust Gap

Qodo's $70M raise and the Reuters Institute trust gap findings are directly connected for engineers in safety-critical systems.

  • AI-generated code is already entering process control contexts: PLC logic, DCS configuration scripts, SCADA HMI code, and safety instrumented system (SIS) function blocks. In most cases it arrives through developer tools that generate code suggestions, not through explicitly "AI systems"
  • The problem Qodo is solving — verifying that AI-generated code actually does what it claims — is a version of the same problem that IEC 61511 (functional safety for process industry SIS) requires engineers to solve through independent verification and validation (V&V)
  • Safety engineers cannot accept AI-generated control logic at face value. IEC 61511 and AS 61511 both require that safety function code be formally verified, tested, and documented regardless of how it was produced. "An AI wrote it and it looks right" is not a defence in a HAZOP or a post-incident investigation
  • The trust gap finding is rational, not irrational. Experienced safety engineers who distrust AI recommendations and verify them independently are behaving correctly. The risk is over-reliance by less experienced engineers under time pressure
  • Try this: Find the IEC 61511 Part 1 requirements for software V&V. Consider how you would apply those requirements to a scenario where a co-pilot AI suggested a change to a safety function's trip setpoint logic.

R&D and Data Scientists — Open-Source AI Infrastructure Maturing

Mistral's $830M raise to build a Paris data centre is a signal that open-weight AI infrastructure is reaching industrial scale. For engineers running open-source models on-premise for IP-sensitive process data, this is relevant.

  • Open-weight models from Mistral (Mixtral, Mistral Large) are used by some Australian industrial operators who cannot send proprietary process data to US-hosted cloud APIs due to data sovereignty requirements
  • The infrastructure investment means Mistral's model quality and inference performance will continue to improve, keeping open-source options competitive with GPT and Claude for structured data analysis tasks
  • Try this: Test Mistral Large against Claude Sonnet 4.6 on a process data interpretation task — for example, interpreting a time-series of reactor temperature anomalies. Compare response quality and hallucination frequency.

The Bigger Picture

Today's stories converge on a single uncomfortable truth: AI is changing the material conditions of engineering work faster than the institutions that govern it can respond. Job structures, energy markets, safety standards, and trust frameworks are all being stressed simultaneously.

The job wipeout question is not answered by "AI will create new jobs" — that may be true in aggregate, but it is cold comfort for a process engineer whose specific role is being eliminated at a Pilbara site or a Melbourne chemical plant. The honest answer is that some roles will disappear, some will transform, and new roles will emerge that don't exist yet. The engineers who shape that transition rather than being shaped by it are the ones who understand both what AI can do and what it cannot.

The Tasmania power deal and Qodo code verification story are reminders that AI is not just software — it is physical infrastructure with physical consequences. It consumes electricity, generates heat, requires cooling water, and produces code that controls real equipment. Chemical engineers are exactly the people who understand those physical realities. That knowledge is not obsolete. It is, if anything, more valuable in a world where AI systems need to operate in physical environments.

The trust gap is the right response to an immature technology in a safety-critical domain. Trust should be earned through demonstrated reliability, formal verification, and transparent failure modes. That is what engineering standards already require. The challenge for the next generation of chemical engineers is building AI systems that deserve that trust — not assuming it.
