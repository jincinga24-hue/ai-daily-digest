OpenAI Raises $3B from Retail Investors in $122B Fund Raise

OpenAI has for the first time opened a portion of its ongoing capital raise to retail investors, closing a $3B tranche as part of its broader $122B fundraising round. Retail participation in a pre-IPO OpenAI raise is a structural first — until now, access was limited to institutional and accredited investors. The move is widely read as either an IPO signal or a retail liquidity mechanism designed to build public shareholder support before a listing. The $122B valuation makes OpenAI one of the highest-valued private companies in history.

Salesforce Announces 30 New AI Features for Slack

Salesforce has announced a major AI makeover for Slack, adding 30 new AI-powered features including autonomous agents that can run inside Slack channels, AI-generated meeting summaries, automated action item assignment, and cross-channel incident tracking. The suite targets enterprise teams and positions Slack as an AI-native coordination layer rather than a messaging tool. The features are built on Salesforce's Agentforce platform and integrate with CRM data, enabling agents to surface relevant customer and project context directly in channels.

  • Source: TechCrunch — Salesforce Slack AI makeover

Mercor Cyberattack via LiteLLM Open-Source Compromise

Mercor, an AI-powered hiring platform, disclosed a cyberattack traced to a supply chain compromise of LiteLLM — the popular open-source library used to route requests across multiple LLM providers. The attackers injected malicious code into a LiteLLM dependency that allowed credential exfiltration from any application running the compromised version. LiteLLM is widely used across the AI industry as a unified API gateway for GPT, Claude, Gemini, and open-source models. The attack is a first significant supply chain incident targeting core AI infrastructure tooling.

  • Source: TechCrunch — Mercor cyberattack via LiteLLM

Runway Launches $10M Fund for Early-Stage AI Startups

Runway, the AI video and creative tools company, has launched a $10M early-stage fund targeting AI startups building on top of foundation models. The fund will back 20–25 companies at pre-seed stage with checks of $250K–$500K and prioritises teams building specialised AI tooling, vertical AI applications, and AI-native creative platforms. The move positions Runway as both a product company and an investor in the AI application layer ecosystem.

Plant Operations & Shift Teams — Slack AI Features for Incident Coordination

Salesforce's 30 new AI features for Slack are aimed at enterprise teams, but the specific capabilities announced have direct relevance to plant operations contexts where Slack (or Teams) is used for shift coordination.

  • AI-generated shift handover summaries are a significant operational capability. Shift handover is one of the highest-risk periods in process plant operation — information that doesn't transfer correctly between shifts contributes to incidents. An AI that summarises open work orders, active process deviations, and outstanding actions from the outgoing shift reduces that risk
  • Automated action item assignment from channel discussions means that if a process issue is discussed and actions agreed in a Slack channel, the AI can extract those commitments and assign them without manual tracking. For maintenance coordination and turnaround planning, this compresses the overhead of tracking open items
  • Cross-channel incident tracking lets operations, maintenance, and HSE teams work in separate channels while the AI surfaces relevant information across all of them during an active incident — reducing the siloed communication problem that complicates emergency response
  • The caveat: AI-generated summaries of safety-critical discussions must be reviewed, not treated as authoritative records. A Slack AI that misses a critical process deviation from the handover summary is worse than no summary. These tools reduce overhead but do not replace human verification of safety-critical information transfer
  • Try this: Draft a prompt for an AI assistant that could generate a structured shift handover summary from a Slack channel transcript. What fields would you require? What verification step would you build in?

Any ChemE Team Using Open-Source AI Tooling — LiteLLM Supply Chain Attack Is a Direct Warning

The Mercor/LiteLLM supply chain attack is the most technically important security story of the week for chemical engineering teams using AI infrastructure.

  • LiteLLM is the library of choice for many engineering teams building internal AI tools — it provides a unified API layer that lets you route prompts to GPT, Claude, Gemini, or local open-weight models through one interface. If your team has built an internal AI tool (for simulation interpretation, document Q&A, or HAZOP assistance), there is a meaningful chance it depends on LiteLLM
  • Supply chain attacks on open-source packages work by compromising a dependency — not the main package, but something it imports — and injecting malicious code. The malicious code in this case exfiltrated API credentials and session tokens. For an industrial operator, that means API keys for Claude or GPT, and potentially access tokens for internal systems
  • The immediate action for any team using LiteLLM is to check the version pinned in your dependencies, review the LiteLLM security advisory, rotate any API keys that may have been exposed, and audit your dependency chain for any transitive dependencies with recent unexpected updates
  • Safety-critical systems that depend on AI output must have isolated network architectures. API calls to external LLM providers should never originate from the same network segment as process control systems or safety instrumented systems. The LiteLLM attack would not be able to compromise a DCS if the AI tooling was properly network-segmented
  • This attack will not be the last. Open-source AI tooling is now high-value enough to be a supply chain attack target. Every team using open-source AI libraries needs a dependency audit cadence — not just for the main packages, but for transitive dependencies as well
  • Try this: If your team uses any AI tooling in a technical context, run pip list --outdated or npm audit right now. Find out who owns your dependency update process. If the answer is "nobody," that is your gap.

The Bigger Picture

Today's five stories each represent a different layer of the AI stack maturing simultaneously. OpenAI opening to retail investors is the capital layer opening up. Anthropic's month is the model and product layer accelerating. Slack's AI makeover is the enterprise workflow layer activating. The LiteLLM attack is the security and infrastructure layer under stress. Runway's fund is the ecosystem layer expanding.

For chemical engineers, the pattern that matters is this: AI is no longer a research topic or a proof-of-concept technology — it is operational infrastructure. It sits in enterprise tools your colleagues use daily. It is targeted by sophisticated attackers. It is backed by institutional and now retail capital at a scale that signals long-term commitment. And it is coming to Australia, with Anthropic's Sydney office as the first direct footprint.

The LiteLLM attack is the cautionary note that should not be ignored. Every layer of the AI stack that matures also becomes a higher-value attack surface. Chemical engineers who build or oversee AI tools in industrial contexts cannot treat open-source library security as someone else's problem. The consequence of a credential exfiltration in a process control context is not a data breach notice — it is potential unauthorised access to systems that control physical equipment.

The right posture is neither uncritical adoption nor reflexive avoidance. It is deliberate integration with proper security hygiene, network architecture, and operational verification. That is exactly what chemical engineering training prepares you for — systematic risk analysis applied to new technology.
