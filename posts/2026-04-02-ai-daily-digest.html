Anthropic's Mass DMCA Takedown Accidentally Kills Thousands of Open-Source Repos

Anthropic filed a large-scale DMCA takedown campaign on GitHub targeting repositories hosting leaked source code for its unreleased "Mythos" model. The takedowns were automated or insufficiently reviewed — and swept up thousands of legitimate open-source repositories that happened to contain similar code patterns, keywords, or were collateral targets of over-broad takedown notices. Affected projects include AI tooling libraries, research implementations, and community-maintained forks that had no connection to the Mythos leak. GitHub's DMCA response process requires immediate takedown upon receipt of a valid notice, meaning repositories were offline before operators could contest the removal. As of publication, Anthropic has acknowledged the over-reach and is working to restore affected repositories, but the CI/CD pipelines and dependency chains of projects built on those repos broke overnight.

Cognichip Raises $60M for AI-Designed Chips

Cognichip, a San Francisco-based startup building AI systems that design semiconductor chips, closed a $60M Series A. The company's platform uses reinforcement learning and generative design to optimise chip layouts, power profiles, and thermal characteristics — tasks that currently take teams of human silicon engineers months to complete. AI-designed chips are already demonstrating 15–25% power efficiency gains over human-designed equivalents for specific workloads. The recursive implication is direct: the AI chips that power the next generation of AI models will themselves be designed by AI. Cognichip is targeting data centre accelerators and edge inference chips as its primary markets.

  • Source: TechCrunch — Cognichip raises $60M for AI-designed chips

Meta's Natural Gas Consumption for AI Data Centres Could Power South Dakota

New analysis of Meta's energy filings reveals that the company's natural gas consumption for AI data centre operations has reached a scale equivalent to powering the entire state of South Dakota — approximately 900,000 residents. The data centres supporting Meta's AI training runs (Llama 4 and successor models) and inference infrastructure are now among the largest single industrial gas consumers in the United States. The energy profile is driven by the power density requirements of GPU clusters, backup generation infrastructure, and cooling. The analysis arrives as AI companies negotiate long-term power purchase agreements with utilities at a scale that directly competes with industrial users, municipalities, and grid stability commitments.

Who's Impacted Today

ChemE Teams Using GitHub-Hosted AI Tooling — Your Pipeline Is a Single DMCA Notice Away From Breaking

The Anthropic DMCA takedown is primarily a story about IP enforcement gone wrong — but its mechanism is a direct operational risk for any chemical engineering team that has built workflows on open-source AI libraries hosted on GitHub.

  • If your team built an internal AI tool — a simulation interpreter, a HAZOP assistant, a document Q&A system — using open-source libraries like LangChain, LlamaIndex, LiteLLM, or community-maintained transformer implementations, those tools depend on GitHub-hosted packages. A DMCA takedown on any of those repos breaks your dependency resolution overnight
  • This is not a theoretical risk. The Anthropic incident took down thousands of repos that had nothing to do with the Mythos leak — collateral damage from automated, over-broad enforcement. The same mechanism could affect any popular open-source AI library if a rights holder files a sufficiently wide takedown notice
  • The correct mitigation is dependency pinning and private mirroring. Pin your dependencies to specific versions and store them in a private package registry (Artifactory, AWS CodeArtifact, or a simple private PyPI mirror). If the public GitHub repo disappears, your CI/CD still resolves from your mirror. This is standard practice in industrial software environments — it needs to apply to AI tooling as well
  • The broader pattern: open-source AI tooling is now high-value enough to attract IP enforcement, supply chain attacks (yesterday's LiteLLM story), and accelerated deprecation cycles. Engineering teams that treat open-source AI libraries like stable infrastructure are taking on risk they may not have priced in
  • Try this: Identify the three most critical open-source AI libraries your team depends on. Check whether they are pinned in your dependency files. If they are not — or if you have no private mirror — that is your immediate gap.

Process Simulation & Digital Twins — AI-Designed Chips Are the Hardware Layer for Real-Time Twins

Cognichip's $60M raise is a hardware story, but its consequence for process engineering is concrete: AI-designed chips are the hardware layer that will make real-time digital twins in mining, LNG, and refining economically viable at scale.

  • Real-time digital twins for process plants require continuous simulation — running Aspen HYSYS or equivalent models against live sensor data at update frequencies of seconds, not hours. Current GPU-based inference infrastructure can do this, but at energy and cost profiles that limit deployment to tier-one operators. A 15–25% power efficiency gain from AI-designed chips translates directly to lower operating costs for AI-assisted process control
  • The recursive dynamic matters: the next generation of chips designed by Cognichip-style AI will be optimised for AI inference workloads specifically — including the mixture-of-experts and transformer architectures that underpin process simulation co-pilots. This is not a generic compute improvement; it is targeted optimisation of the exact hardware running the AI tools in your workflow
  • For LNG and mining operators, edge inference is increasingly important — running AI models on-site rather than sending data to cloud APIs, for latency, cost, and data sovereignty reasons. AI-designed edge chips optimised for inference will accelerate this trend. Woodside's digital operations centre and BHP's Pilbara AI programs are both constrained by current edge hardware economics
  • The 18–24 month horizon for Cognichip's first commercial chips aligns with the timeline for the next generation of process simulation AI tools. Engineers specifying hardware for new digital twin infrastructure today should plan for a step-change in available edge inference capability within that window
  • Try this: Look up the current hardware specifications for Woodside's digital operations platform or BHP's AI-assisted Pilbara control systems. Identify what the current processing constraints are and where a 20% power efficiency improvement would change the deployment economics.

Australian Energy & Industrial Operators — Data Centres Are Now Competing With You for Power

Meta's South Dakota-scale natural gas consumption is a US story, but the energy dynamic it represents is arriving in Australia faster than most operators have priced in — and connects directly to last week's Tasmania AI power deal story.

  • Australian AI data centre buildout is accelerating. Microsoft's $5B Australian data centre commitment, Google's Melbourne expansion, and the Tasmanian power deal announced last week all represent long-term industrial-scale energy demand entering the Australian grid. Data centres are not intermittent consumers — they run at high load factors 24/7, competing for the same baseload power that aluminium smelters, LNG compressor trains, and water treatment plants depend on
  • The mechanism for industrial operators is through electricity pricing and grid availability. AEMO's forecasting models have been updated to include AI data centre load, but the pace of new commitments is outrunning those models. Operators with energy-intensive processes — electrolysis, compression, separation — should be reviewing their long-term power purchase agreements against the updated demand projections
  • Natural gas specifically is relevant for Australian LNG operators. Meta's gas consumption story is driven by backup generation and peaking capacity. In Australia, where gas is both an export commodity and a domestic industrial feedstock, AI infrastructure competing for gas supply adds a new demand variable to domestic gas pricing — relevant for any operation with gas offtake agreements coming up for renegotiation
  • The sustainability angle: chemical engineers in environmental and sustainability roles will increasingly be asked to model data centre energy demand as part of scope 3 emissions accounting for clients. Understanding the energy profile of AI infrastructure — GPU density, cooling requirements, backup generation — is becoming a relevant technical competency
  • Try this: Look up AEMO's latest Integrated System Plan update (ISP 2026). Find the section on data centre demand forecasting. Compare it to the AI infrastructure commitments announced in Australia in Q1 2026 alone. Note the gap.

The Bigger Picture

Today's three stories share a common thread: the consequences of AI scaling are propagating into infrastructure layers that chemical engineers manage, depend on, or operate within.

The Anthropic DMCA incident is a reminder that open-source AI tooling is no longer a low-stakes, community-governed commons. It is commercially valuable IP that large companies will defend aggressively — and the collateral damage of that defence lands on anyone who built workflows on top of it without proper dependency management. The engineering discipline of supply chain integrity applies to software dependencies just as it applies to physical feedstocks.

Cognichip's chip design AI closes a loop that most engineers have not yet noticed: the AI tools in your simulation software will eventually run on hardware that was itself designed by AI. The performance and cost trajectory of those tools is therefore tied not just to model capability improvements (which get most of the attention) but to hardware efficiency improvements that compound on top of them.

Meta's energy footprint is the most directly confronting story for anyone in Australian industrial operations. The energy transition was already reshaping the economics of energy-intensive chemical processes. AI infrastructure adds a new large-scale industrial demand class to that transition — one that is growing faster than grid planning cycles can accommodate. That is not an abstract concern. It is an operational constraint that will show up in electricity prices, gas availability, and grid stability for Australian operators within this decade.

The pattern: AI is not just a tool layer. It is a physical infrastructure layer with energy, material, and supply chain consequences that are now large enough to affect the environments where chemical engineers work.
